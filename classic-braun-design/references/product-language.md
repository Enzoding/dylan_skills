# Classic Braun Product Language

Use this reference to make digital work feel like a classic Braun product. Use these traits selectively; a strong result usually combines 2-4 dominant traits with restraint.

## Source Anchors

Classic Braun language is associated with radios, record players, audio components, clocks, watches, calculators, lighters, and compact household devices shaped by Braun's postwar design culture and Dieter Rams-era product systems. Do not name model numbers in user-facing output unless asked.

Think in shape families instead:
- Radio face
- Audio component
- Pocket device
- Calculator keypad
- Clock or watch dial
- Equipment label
- Control panel
- Module stack
- Grille or perforation field
- Clear protective lid

## Geometry

- Use flat, rectilinear product faces with precise proportions.
- Prefer low-radius rectangles, circular controls, circular indicators, horizontal slots, perforation grids, and fine divider lines.
- Use strong orthogonal alignment. Elements should feel measured, not casually placed.
- Use modules that can stack, align, or repeat like components in a product system.
- Use circles for controls, indicators, timers, meters, and focal readouts.
- Use large surfaces calmly. A product face can be mostly quiet if the control logic is crisp.

Avoid:
- Balloon-like rounded cards.
- Organic blobs and decorative abstract shapes.
- Excessive diagonals, chaotic asymmetry, or expressive poster composition.

## Color

Use ranges, not fixed tokens:
- Large surfaces: warm white, off-white, porcelain white, light grey, cool grey.
- Secondary surfaces: mid grey, graphite, charcoal, matte black.
- Material accents: aluminum silver, pale metal, clear acrylic, occasional light wood.
- Functional accents: yellow, red, green, orange, or blue in very small amounts for status, selection, warnings, power, time, or active controls.

Rules:
- Let neutral surfaces dominate.
- Use one accent color as a functional signal, not decoration.
- Keep contrast sufficient for accessibility.
- Make black feel matte and engineered, not glossy luxury.

Avoid:
- Large gradients.
- Neon glow.
- Purple-blue SaaS palettes.
- Sepia, aged paper, film grain, or vintage poster color.
- Trendy beige-only lifestyle palettes.

## Materials

Translate physical materials into digital surfaces:
- Matte plastic -> flat warm neutral panels with subtle borders.
- Painted metal -> clean white or grey fields with precise edges.
- Brushed aluminum -> restrained silver/grey strips, rails, dividers, or control bands.
- Clear acrylic -> transparent overlay only when it reads as a protective layer, not glassmorphism.
- Light wood side panels -> optional warm side rail or frame, used sparingly.

Avoid:
- Heavy skeuomorphic shadows.
- Leather, screws, fake bolts, worn texture, or distressed surfaces.
- Frosted glass cards and glossy reflections.

## Typography

- Use a clear neutral sans-serif.
- Favor small, precise labels over expressive headline theatrics.
- Use all-caps sparingly for equipment labels, section captions, and control legends.
- Use tabular numbers for measurements, time, counts, prices, metrics, and scales.
- Keep letter spacing normal unless a label genuinely needs technical spacing.
- Use labels close to the controls they describe.

Avoid:
- Oversized marketing headlines as the primary style device.
- Retro display fonts.
- Decorative Bauhaus type treatments.
- Tiny low-contrast type that only looks refined in screenshots.

## Control Language

Classic Braun controls are obvious, tactile, and calm:
- Circular knobs and dials.
- Segmented switches.
- Calculator-like buttons.
- Sliders with tick marks.
- Small status lamps.
- Numbered or labeled control groups.
- Perforation fields and speaker-like grille patterns.
- Fine scale marks and measurement labels.

Use controls to support real digital behavior. Do not add fake controls that do nothing unless they are clearly decorative and do not confuse interaction.

## Composition

- Separate display areas from control areas.
- Use faceplate logic: a clear front surface containing readout, controls, labels, and status.
- Align content to a strict grid.
- Keep groups compact and intentional.
- Use borders, fine lines, or color planes to create product zones.
- Let repeated controls share size and rhythm.

## What This Is Not

- Not generic minimal SaaS.
- Not Apple product-page minimalism.
- Not cyberpunk, neon, black-glass tech UI.
- Not nostalgic retro poster design.
- Not Bauhaus red/yellow/blue geometry.
- Not mechanical skeuomorphism.
- Not a museum essay about Dieter Rams.

Classic Braun style should feel clean, engineered, readable, useful, and physically plausible.
