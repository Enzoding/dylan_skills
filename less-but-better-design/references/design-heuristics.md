# Design Heuristics

Use these moves while designing or reviewing a digital interface through a "less but better" lens.

## Start Here

- Identify the primary task before choosing visual treatments.
- Rank information by user value, not by what is easiest to make visually dramatic.
- Design the normal, empty, loading, error, disabled, and success states early.
- Preserve the user's requested brand and emotional direction, then remove noise inside that direction.
- Make the first screen communicate what the product is, what matters, and what can be done.

## Concrete Moves

- Strengthen hierarchy before adding color.
- Improve alignment and spacing before adding decoration.
- Use fewer emphasis styles; make each one mean something.
- Turn decorative cards into layout structure when the content does not need framing.
- Reduce secondary visual weight instead of hiding secondary information.
- Make controls look like controls.
- Keep icons, type, radius, border, and shadow systems consistent.
- Replace vague promotional copy with specific product truth.
- Use motion for orientation, feedback, and continuity, not ambient spectacle.
- Keep dense interfaces dense enough for work, but ordered enough for scanning.
- Let white space separate ideas; do not use it to avoid making hard hierarchy decisions.

## Common Anti-Patterns

- "Premium" achieved through low contrast, tiny type, and under-explained controls.
- "Minimal" achieved by removing feedback, navigation, labels, or context.
- "Modern" achieved by defaulting to gradients, glass, glow, floating cards, and large empty hero sections.
- "Immersive" achieved by letting imagery or animation overpower the main task.
- "AI" achieved through abstract magic language, vague glow effects, or inflated claims.
- "Professional" achieved by making the interface cold, generic, and thin on workflow detail.
- "Brand expression" achieved by making every element compete for attention.
- "Clean" achieved by hiding complexity instead of organizing it.

## Pushback Rules

- Push back gently when a requested visual move adds noise but does not harm use.
- Push back firmly when a move damages usability, accessibility, clarity, honesty, or task completion.
- Always preserve the user's intent in the alternative: keep the mood, reduce the cost.
- Prefer "I would make this quieter because..." over broad claims that a style is wrong.

## Final Pass

Before finishing, ask:

- What is the one thing this interface must help the user do?
- Is anything louder than that task without earning its volume?
- Are states, limits, and consequences visible?
- Is any copy promising more than the product can deliver?
- Does the design still work with real content, long labels, small screens, and repeated use?
- What can be removed, softened, grouped, or clarified now?
