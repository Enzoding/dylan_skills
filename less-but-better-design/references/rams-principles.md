# Rams Principles for Digital Interfaces

Use this reference to translate Rams's product-design principles into interface decisions. Treat the principles as decision tools, not a mandatory report.

## 1. Innovative

Use technology to make the product more useful, understandable, or humane. Do not add novelty for its own sake.

Digital translation:
- Prefer meaningful interaction improvements over fashionable effects.
- Use AI, animation, 3D, or advanced UI only when it improves the task.
- Let new capability change the workflow, not just the visual surface.

Ask:
- What can this interface do better because of the available technology?
- Is this feature genuinely useful or just impressive in a demo?

## 2. Useful

A design exists to be used. Functional, psychological, and aesthetic needs all matter.

Digital translation:
- Start from the user's primary task.
- Give important actions, status, and information enough visibility.
- Do not remove necessary controls or context in the name of minimalism.

Ask:
- What is the user trying to complete?
- What must be immediately visible for that task to succeed?

## 3. Aesthetic

Beauty is part of usefulness because daily tools affect attention, confidence, and well-being.

Digital translation:
- Use proportion, rhythm, contrast, and hierarchy to make the interface comfortable and trustworthy.
- Avoid confusing "tasteful" with low contrast, tiny type, or emptiness.
- Make dense tools feel ordered rather than decorated.

Ask:
- Does the visual system make the task easier to enter and sustain?
- Does the design feel resolved at real content density?

## 4. Understandable

Good design clarifies structure and makes use self-evident where possible.

Digital translation:
- Make navigation, available actions, states, and consequences clear.
- Use labels, grouping, affordances, and feedback to reduce guessing.
- Do not rely on explanatory text to compensate for unclear structure.

Ask:
- Can a new user tell what this is and what to do next?
- Are controls discoverable without visual clutter?

## 5. Unobtrusive

Useful products are tools, not self-advertisements.

Digital translation:
- Let primary tasks dominate over decoration, brand flourish, and motion.
- Keep repeated-use interfaces calm and scannable.
- Save drama for moments where the brief truly calls for it.

Ask:
- Is the interface helping the user act, or asking to be admired?
- Which visual moments should become quieter?

## 6. Honest

Design must not make a product seem more powerful, valuable, or certain than it is.

Digital translation:
- Do not exaggerate product capability in copy or visuals.
- Show uncertainty, limits, pricing, latency, data freshness, and failure states clearly.
- Avoid fake depth, fake activity, fake precision, or misleading hierarchy.

Ask:
- What promise does the screen imply?
- Is that promise true under real conditions?

## 7. Long-Lasting

Avoid chasing fashion when the product needs to endure.

Digital translation:
- Favor stable patterns, typography, spacing, and interaction models.
- Use trends only when they support brand, audience, or campaign goals.
- Design systems should survive more than one launch cycle.

Ask:
- Will this still feel appropriate after the current visual trend fades?
- What parts would be embarrassing or expensive to maintain later?

## 8. Thorough

Nothing important should feel arbitrary.

Digital translation:
- Resolve details across breakpoints, states, edge cases, and content lengths.
- Align icon style, control sizing, spacing increments, and interaction feedback.
- Treat empty, error, loading, disabled, selected, focus, and hover states as first-class design.

Ask:
- Which states have not been designed yet?
- What looks accidental?

## 9. Environmentally Friendly

In digital work, resource responsibility includes performance, maintainability, and attention cost.

Digital translation:
- Avoid heavy assets and unnecessary animation when they do not serve the experience.
- Prefer simpler implementation when it produces equal or better user value.
- Reduce visual and cognitive pollution.

Ask:
- What is this design costing in load time, complexity, maintenance, or attention?
- Can the same value be delivered with less waste?

## 10. As Little Design As Possible

Less, but better: concentrate on essential aspects and remove non-essentials.

Digital translation:
- Remove what does not support use, meaning, feedback, brand intent, or orientation.
- Keep necessary complexity; organize it instead of hiding it.
- Make restraint feel full of intent, not empty or unfinished.

Ask:
- What can be removed without weakening the task?
- What must remain because it carries meaning or utility?
