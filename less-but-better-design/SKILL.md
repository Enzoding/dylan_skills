# Less But Better Design

## Purpose

Use this skill to improve design taste and decision-making, not to apply a fixed visual skin. Translate Rams/Braun principles into digital interfaces that are useful, understandable, honest, restrained, durable, and carefully detailed while still respecting the user's original brand, audience, mood, and functional goals.

Do not make every output look like Braun hardware. Let the user's brief lead the visual direction; use this skill to remove noise, sharpen hierarchy, and keep the design accountable to real use.

## Working Method

1. Infer the real use context before changing visuals: who uses this, what they need to do, what must be understood quickly, and what could distract or mislead them.
2. Preserve the user's requested style, domain, and emotional target unless it damages usefulness, clarity, honesty, accessibility, or core task completion.
3. Establish the essential hierarchy first: primary task, secondary actions, supporting information, status, feedback, and edge states.
4. Remove or weaken elements that do not support function, meaning, navigation, feedback, brand intent, or emotional orientation.
5. Keep necessary complexity visible and well organized. Do not confuse "less" with missing information, hidden affordances, or weak feedback.
6. Prefer calm precision over spectacle: stable spacing, aligned controls, clear type scale, explicit states, consistent iconography, and restrained visual emphasis.
7. Before finishing, quietly check the design against the most relevant principles. Fix issues directly. Only explain tradeoffs when they affect the user's decision.

## Design Stance

- Treat usefulness as the root of beauty. A screen that is hard to use is not refined.
- Make structure legible. The interface should reveal what it is, what matters, and what can be done.
- Be honest. Do not use copy, motion, contrast, or layout to exaggerate capability or hide limitations.
- Be unobtrusive where the user needs control, attention, and repeated use. Let the product serve rather than perform.
- Be long-lasting. Avoid fashion effects that will date the interface unless the brief explicitly asks for a short-lived campaign or expressive art direction.
- Be thorough in details: loading, empty, error, disabled, selected, hover, focus, overflow, mobile, and dense-content states deserve care.
- Be resource-conscious in digital terms: avoid heavy media, gratuitous animation, and ornamental complexity that harms performance or maintainability.

## Output Behavior

Do not emit a ritualized named checklist or numeric score. Let the design output carry the principles. If useful, briefly mention concrete choices such as what was removed, clarified, softened, or preserved.

When pushing back, be advisory rather than dogmatic. Respect explicit user direction, name the risk, and offer a better alternative that keeps the user's intent.
